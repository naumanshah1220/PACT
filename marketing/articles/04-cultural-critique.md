# Every Online Platform Is a Prisoner's Dilemma. We're All Losing.

*Target: Twitter/X, Substack, contrarian-friendly publications. Voice: provocateur, sharp, deliberately uncomfortable. The kind of piece people share to say "this explains everything."*

---

Let me describe a system to you.

Millions of participants. Each individual is better off making certain choices — choices that, aggregated, make everyone worse off. There is no coordination mechanism. There is no authority that can enforce cooperation. And the system is specifically designed to maximise the frequency of the individually rational but collectively destructive choice.

I'm describing Twitter. I'm describing Facebook. I'm describing the comments section, the group chat, the office Slack, the neighbourhood forum, the national conversation.

I'm describing most of modern public life.

---

Every engagement-optimised platform is running the same experiment: what happens when you give people a microphone and a scoreboard?

The answer, which should surprise no one who's ever sat through an introductory game theory lecture, is that people defect. They post the outrage bait instead of the nuanced take. They pile on, because piling on is low-cost and occasionally rewarding. They screenshot the worst interpretation of someone's words because screenshots spread further than context.

Each individual choice is, in a narrow sense, rational. Each individual choice, aggregated, produces a discourse that nobody enjoys — including, and this is the important part, the people making it.

Surveys consistently show that people feel worse after using social media. They keep using it anyway. This is not a failure of willpower. This is the Nash equilibrium of a system optimised for individual engagement at the expense of collective experience. Individual rationality. Collective catastrophe. The Prisoner's Dilemma, running at scale, continuously, with no end state.

---

The original Prisoner's Dilemma was a one-shot game. Two strangers. One decision. No future.

What game theory has known since the 1980s is that iteration changes everything. When you'll play again with the same person, your reputation matters. The shadow of future interactions disciplines present behaviour. Robert Axelrod showed that "Tit for Tat" — cooperate first, mirror whatever your opponent does after that — outcompetes every other strategy in repeated play. Not because it's nice. Because betrayal has consequences when you'll face the same player again.

This is why small towns have lower crime rates than anonymous cities. This is why professional communities have norms. This is why neighbourhoods with stable populations maintain public goods better than ones with high turnover. Reputation is the mechanism by which cooperation becomes rational.

Engagement-optimised platforms have, through design, converted an iterated game into a one-shot game. You post to a different audience each time. The feedback loop between your behaviour and your future interactions is broken or absent. The mechanism by which cooperation becomes rational has been removed.

We didn't choose this. Nobody voted for it. It's just what happens when you optimise for clicks.

---

The solutions people propose — better moderation, healthier algorithms, digital literacy education, government regulation — are all real and worth pursuing. They are also all attempts to impose cooperative equilibria on top of a system whose architecture actively resists them.

You cannot solve a structural Prisoner's Dilemma with individual virtue. You can only solve it by changing the payoff structure.

---

PACT won't fix Twitter. Nothing will fix Twitter.

But it gives you something that most digital experiences don't: the specific, physical, visceral experience of the Prisoner's Dilemma in real time. Two players. A real wager. An open channel. A simultaneous reveal.

You feel the trust decision differently when it's your gold on the line and someone is typing to you right now. You feel the sting of betrayal as information — not as an abstraction about human nature, but as a specific fact about a specific person in a specific moment.

Once you feel it in a five-minute game, you start recognising it everywhere. In the way a colleague frames an email. In the way a politician phrases a promise. In the way the platform is designed to make you feel something that keeps you scrolling.

Seeing the game doesn't make you immune. But it's a start.
