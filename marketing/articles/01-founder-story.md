# I Built a Game Where Strangers Betray Each Other. It's Changed How I See Everyone.

*Target: Medium, personal newsletter, Twitter/X thread. Voice: personal, vulnerable, introspective.*

---

Here's the thing nobody tells you about building a social game: you become your own test subject.

I was maybe three weeks into development when a friend agreed to playtest PACT with me. We'd been friends for seven years. We knew each other's habits, passwords, coffee orders. We loaded up the duel room, typed our pleasantries, made our pledges in the chat. Then we both hit the final button.

He betrayed me.

He *apologised* immediately. Said he got curious. Wanted to see what would happen. But here's what stuck with me: in the thirty seconds between when I made my choice and when the results came in, I had *never been so certain* of anything. Of course he'd pledge. We were friends. Why would he betray me?

That certainty, and then the cold slap of being wrong about it — that's the game. That's what I'd been building. And it hit differently when it was real.

---

The Prisoner's Dilemma is one of the oldest problems in game theory. Two people are arrested. They can't communicate. Each can cooperate (stay silent) or defect (betray the other). If they both cooperate, light sentences for both. If one betrays and the other doesn't, the betrayer walks free and the cooperator gets the maximum. If both betray, both get hard time.

The "rational" choice — for each individual — is always to betray. Regardless of what your partner does, you come out better by defecting. And yet, if both players follow this logic, both lose more than if both had trusted each other.

It's a paradox at the heart of human civilisation. We face it in climate negotiations, nuclear deterrence, office politics, and whether to leave a tip at a restaurant you'll never visit again. You've been playing it your whole life without knowing it had a name.

I built PACT because I wanted to *feel* that paradox, not just understand it.

---

The design is simple. You wager gold. You match with an opponent — human or one of the game's medieval personalities. You have time to talk. You can plead, bluff, philosophise, threaten, or stay silent. Then, at a moment of your choosing, you both invoke the Seal. Results are revealed simultaneously.

What I didn't expect when building it: the *conversations*. The conversations are the game. People write paragraphs. They reason, they negotiate, they confess anxieties. Strangers end up having more honest exchanges in five minutes of a PACT duel than in a year of Twitter back-and-forth.

Because the stakes are clear. Because the clock is running. Because you know, on some level, that what you say right now will be weighed against a decision you can't take back.

---

A few things I've noticed watching hundreds of test duels:

People who pledge first in the chat tend to be more trusted — but also more frequently betrayed. Their openness signals vulnerability.

People who say "I always cooperate" are often lying. The ones who say nothing often mean it.

The bot called Lord Harkon — who *always* betrays, and tells you so directly — gets pledged to more than almost any other opponent. People think they can outwit him. They never do.

The game doesn't teach you what to do. It teaches you what you already believe — about people, about trust, about whether the world is fundamentally cooperative or zero-sum.

---

Something I didn't anticipate: the specific feeling of being the betrayer.

The first time I chose to betray someone in a test duel — a stranger, virtual gold, nothing real at stake — I felt something. Not guilt exactly. More like the quiet awareness of having made a certain kind of choice, and knowing that the other person was about to discover it at the exact same moment I confirmed it.

The reveal is simultaneous. You don't know their result until they know yours. That shared moment of finding out — together, at the same time — is designed to feel like something. I think it does.

Being betrayed by my friend felt different from being betrayed by a stranger. It felt like information. Like I'd learned something true that I wished I hadn't.

Maybe that's the game. Maybe that's the whole point.
